    Parts and functions of digital cameras

    • 1. Shutter Button Pressing this button will release the shutter and take a picture
    • 2. Main Dial A cogwheel that adjusts the ISO, aperture, and shutter speed setting
    • 3. Mode Dial A small cogwheel that switches different modes of the camera
    • 4. Flash The light that only fires when taking pictures, is always located on the top or front of the camera
    • 5. Flash Button Open the flash before triggered
    • 6. Lens Focus Mode SwitchSwitch betweenAutofocusocus) and MF(Manual focus)
    • 7. Lens Release Button Unlocks the lens mount
    • 8. Lens Adjust the focal length
    • 9. Back
    • 10. Viewfinder A small window that previews the shot by framing the subject that you want to take a photo of. Also, it will show some detail that you set, for example, ISO, aperture, shutter speed

    • 12. Cross Keys Directional buttons
    • 13. Erase Button Delete any unwanted images
    • 14. Play Button Playing back the image that you captured

    • 17. Movie Shooting Button Start video recording after pressing the button
    • 18. Battery Compartment Place that put the battery
    • 19. Card Slot A place that inserts the memory card

    How is the photo being captured?

    • 1. Light enters the camera By pressing the camera's shutter button, the aperture opens at the front of the camera, and light streams in through the lens.
    • 2. Focus the light through the lens Incoming light is focused through the lens and onto the image sensor at the back of the camera. The lens might have various focuses that photographers can use to control the movable elements, adjust them, and let the light focus on the image plane.
    • 3. Capture the light with an image sensor The two main image sensors used today are charged-coupled device (CCD) and complementary mental-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) sensor. They are light-sensitive areas made of crystal silicon. It absorbs photons and releases electrons through the photoelectric effect. The electrons are stored and transformed, and they accumulate over the length of exposure. However, the quantum efficiency of CCD and CMOS varies, and not every photon that hits the detector will register. If a sensor has a quantum efficiency of 40%, four photons will be detected and converted to electrons for every ten photons. CCD and CMOS sensors normally have about 20 to 50% quantum efficiency. Top-line CCD cameras’ quantum efficiencies can reach over 80%.
    • 4. Analog-to-digital conversion The electrons are then transferred and converted to an analog voltage using an analog-to-digital converter (ADC). ADC transforms the analog charge stored in each pixel into a digital value. It shows the brightness and color intensity of the pixel. The transferred digital value can be further processed through image processing, memory storage, or compression for more efficient storage and transmission.
    • 5. Image processing The image processor adjusts and enhances the digital image captured in real time — the main tasks involved demosai
    • 6. Store image The processed digital image is stored in the memory card inserted in the camera or internal memory.
    • 7. Display and review LCD screen at the back of the camera allows users to view the live view from the image sensor and the image captured.
    • 8. Transfer and sharing As the image is stored in the memory, it can easily be transferred to other devices or memories.

    Hardware architecture

    When the camera captures the scene, light focuses and passes through the Colour Filter Array (CFA). The blurring filter is inserted to reduce the artifacts created during demosaicing and downsampling of the color information of CFA. The visual information is sampled from the image sensor and analog-to-digital converter. It is temporarily stored in the DRAM buffer and goes through the application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC). ASIC also gathers information from microprocessors, which are affected by flash, user interface, and power supply. After gathering all the data, digital data processing operations like demosaicking and image resizing started, and the photos were later delivered to stick memory media like cards. It can also be shown on an LCD screen for review. Through USB and A/V, it can also be displayed on PC and TV interfaces for other purpose. The firmware memory contains the instructions for ASIC and governs its operations. It provides the interface for adjusting different camera settings like exposure, focus, white balance and image processing that can change the image characteristics, and all the camera functionalities.

    Software architecture

    • System layer: Display text with picture, Flash Control, Automatic timer, Accessing Processor Internal Registers, Inter-Process Communication(IPC) and Operating System Services
    • Application layer: System Controls, Flash Control, Light Control, Contrast Control, Image Preview shooting
    • Function layer: Standard Access to Image Files, Default Settings, Display Colour Settings, Sound Feedback, Time and Date Display, Dot-Matrix or Touchscreen Driver, ADC Output Format and Data Outputs
    • Presentation layer: Image Files Access, Default Settings, Display Colour Adjustment, Audible Feedback, Dot-Matrix or Touchscreen Driver, ADC Output Formatting and Data Processing
    • Control layer: Controller Tasks, Timer Control, Real-Time Control Modules, Control layer
    • Base layer: Timers, Real-Time Clock(RTC), Serial Interfaces(SI), Analog-to-Digital Converters(ADC), USB Port
